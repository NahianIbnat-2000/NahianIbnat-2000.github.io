About

I recently completed an MA in Economics, Data & Policy at Central European University, specializing in Data Science and Finance, alongside an Advanced Certificate in International Political Economy.

My work sits at the intersection of economics, data science, and public policy. I specialize in causal inference, econometrics, machine learning, forecasting, and data engineering, with a focus on turning rigorous analysis into practical tools.

For my master's thesis, I designed and implemented a novel twin-bond difference-in-differences framework to examine whether climate-related news affects pricing in sovereign green bond markets. Alongside the research, I built an interactive ecosystem of tools, including a policy simulation game, a market simulator, and an AI-powered research assistant, to make complex economic insights more accessible and actionable.

I am particularly interested in analytical and quantitative roles in finance, central banking, research, and policy institutions, where strong empirical methods, clear communication, and sound judgment are essential. I am also excited by opportunities to combine economics with AI, building systems that help people explore, understand, and make decisions from complex data.

When Governments Say "Green" — Do Bond Markets Believe Them?

Spring 2026 · MA Thesis · CEU · sovereign green bond greenium, emerging markets

A twin-bond stacked difference-in-differences design (Baker et al. 2022) testing whether climate-news tone shifts the greenium on sovereign green bonds. GDELT V2Tone sentiment, five specifications (M1–M5) with HC3/PCSE errors, event studies, Granger causality, and robustness checks on European pairs. Built end to end: data, models, write-up, and a deployed product layer with a policy game, live simulator, and research-assistant chatbot.

Null across all three hypotheses in the Asian sample · VIX the only robust daily driver (0.35–0.48 bp/SD) · European greeniums of −1.82 bp (Germany) and −2.80 bp (Denmark)
